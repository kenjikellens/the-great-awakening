Operation Paperclip

The secret U.S. recruitment of Nazi scientists, engineers, and technicians following World War II.

Overview

Operation Paperclip was a secret program of the Joint Intelligence Objectives Agency (JIOA) in which more than 1,600 German scientists, engineers, and technicians were taken from former Nazi Germany to the U.S. for government employment after WWII. The primary goal was to deny German scientific expertise and knowledge to the Soviet Union and the United Kingdom, and to bolster U.S. technological capabilities in the Cold War.

Whitewashing Nazi Records

A central aspect of Paperclip was the deliberate "whitewashing" of the recruits' Nazi pasts. Despite President Truman’s directive that no Nazi members or active supporters be recruited, the JIOA bypassed these restrictions by creating false employment histories and removing incriminating evidence from the files of scientists who were known SS members or participants in slave-labor programs. The name "Paperclip" comes from the paperclip attached to these modified files.

Strategic Impact

Recruits from Operation Paperclip became the architects of essential U.S. military and civilian technologies. Wernher von Braun, previously a major in the SS and developer of the V-2 rocket, became the first director of NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center and the chief architect of the Saturn V launch vehicle. Kurt Debus, a V-2 test launch specialist, became the first director of NASA’s Kennedy Space Center. The research argues that the entire U.S. aerospace and biomedical establishment was effectively seeded with technocratic personnel from the Third Reich.

Research Note

This dossier tracks the Technical Intelligence continuity from the V-2 program to the Apollo missions. We focus on the "Scientific Ethics Suspension" — the precedent set where technological progress was prioritized over moral and legal accountability.
