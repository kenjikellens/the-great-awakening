Algorithmic Enshittification

A dossier on the staged deterioration of dominant digital platforms, focusing on how ranking systems, lock-in, and market power let platforms shift value away from users and business customers.

Introduction

The term "enshittification" emerged in 2023 as a compact description of a pattern many internet users already recognized: digital platforms often begin as highly useful services, then become increasingly manipulative, congested, and extractive once they have achieved scale and dependency. The concept is most commonly associated with author and digital rights advocate Cory Doctorow, who used it to describe how platforms first attract users with convenience, then exploit those users to benefit advertisers, merchants, or other business customers, and finally exploit both sides in order to maximize returns to the platform itself.

Although the word is deliberately blunt, the underlying argument is structurally serious. It is not simply that websites "get worse" over time. The thesis is that platform decline follows from market concentration, high switching costs, control over recommendation systems, and technical barriers that prevent users and business customers from leaving without major loss. In that sense, the concept is relevant well beyond social media. It applies to app stores, search engines, marketplaces, streaming services, and cloud-mediated software ecosystems where a single intermediary controls the rules of visibility and access.

Origin of the Concept

Doctorow's 2023 essays used TikTok, Facebook, Amazon, and other large platforms to explain a recurring three-stage lifecycle. In the first stage, a platform behaves generously toward users because growth is the dominant objective. The product is designed to be sticky, responsive, and pleasant enough to build habit. In the second stage, once the user base is sufficiently captive, value is shifted toward business customers. Sellers, creators, and advertisers are offered new reach and new tools, but only because the platform can use the user base as leverage. In the third stage, the platform begins extracting more aggressively from the business side as well, cutting reach, charging more for placement, deteriorating interoperability, and inserting itself more deeply into transactions.

The concept gained traction because it described a recognizable experience that traditional business language often softened. Users tend to notice the symptoms long before they understand the structure: more ads, worse discovery, forced recommendations, buried chronological ordering, degraded customer support, tighter API restrictions, and increasing dependence on opaque ranking systems. Doctorow's contribution was to tie these symptoms to platform governance, not mere bad design.

How the Process Works

Enshittification depends on asymmetry. The platform has far more information than users or sellers about how ranking, discovery, moderation, and monetization actually work. That informational advantage is amplified by technical control. A dominant platform can change recommendation weights, throttle external links, privilege in-house products, deprioritize independent publishers, or narrow API access without providing transparent justification. From the outside, the system still appears open. In practice, the allocation of attention is managed by the platform operator.

Two-sided markets intensify the effect. A seller cannot easily leave a large marketplace if buyers are concentrated there. A creator cannot easily leave a short-video platform if audiences and monetization tools are locked inside it. A user cannot easily leave a messaging or social platform if their social graph is trapped behind proprietary systems. Each side stays because the other side is there, and the platform sits in the middle extracting more value from both. Once a platform reaches that position, product design is no longer mainly about utility. It becomes about optimizing which constituency can be squeezed without triggering mass exit.

Algorithms are central to this model because they let the platform make those reallocations incrementally and invisibly. Instead of announcing that user welfare now matters less, the platform can simply change how feeds are ranked, how search results are sorted, how external links are penalized, or how often sponsored content appears. The deterioration feels gradual and difficult to prove, which is one reason users often experience it as frustration rather than as a clear shift in governance.

Institutional Significance

The concept matters because it links user experience to competition policy. If platforms decay because they are structurally insulated from exit, then the remedy is not merely better consumer advice. It is lower switching costs, stronger interoperability, enforcement against self-preferencing, and constraints on abusive gatekeeper behavior. This is why Doctorow and the Electronic Frontier Foundation connect enshittification to policy tools such as adversarial interoperability, open standards, repairable interfaces, and anti-monopoly enforcement.

Seen this way, enshittification is not only a cultural complaint about "the bad internet." It is a framework for understanding how market power reshapes information environments. Search engines can prioritize paid placement or in-house verticals. Marketplaces can force sellers into their own logistics or advertising stack. Social platforms can reduce organic reach, making participants buy access to the audience they originally helped build. Each of these changes alters the practical conditions of speech, commerce, and discovery.

Debates and Limits

The concept is persuasive because it captures a real pattern, but it should still be used carefully. Not every declining product is enshittified in the specific structural sense. Sometimes a service deteriorates because of poor leadership, technical debt, or a failed business model rather than because a dominant intermediary is reallocating value through lock-in. Likewise, not every algorithmic change is proof of deliberate exploitation. Good dossier work therefore requires attention to evidence such as market concentration, exit barriers, API closures, ranking opacity, and documented self-preferencing.

There is also a risk of turning the concept into a catch-all explanation for every form of digital dissatisfaction. Its value lies in specificity. It works best when the platform controls an ecosystem, depends on two or more locked-in groups, and can degrade outcomes without facing meaningful competitive discipline. Used in that narrower sense, the concept helps explain why many large internet services feel simultaneously indispensable and increasingly hostile.
