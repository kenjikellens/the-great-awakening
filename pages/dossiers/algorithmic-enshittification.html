Algorithmic Enshittification

Analyzing the systematic lifecycle of platform decay and the intentional degradation of digital services for value extraction.

Documented Evidence

The term "enshittification" describes a three-stage lifecycle common among major two-sided digital platforms. According to the framework established by Cory Doctorow, platforms follow a predictable path of value reallocation:

  1. Surplus for Users: Initially, the platform provides high value and utility to users to build a massive base.
  2. Surplus for Business Customers: Once users are locked in, the platform shifts value to business customers (advertisers, sellers) to monetize the user base.
  3. Surplus for Shareholders: Finally, the platform extracts all remaining value from both users and business customers for its own profit, leading to a degraded and often unusable product.

This process is enabled by a lack of competitive pressure, high switching costs, and the use of algorithms to "twiddle" platform parameters in real-time to favor the platform's own bottom line over the user experience.

Narrative Context

In the context of information control, enshittification is viewed as a mechanism to force users into "digital gulags" or "super-apps." By systematically breaking the open and independent internet, platforms ensure that all digital interactions occur within highly-monitored and algorithmic-controlled environments.

Researchers focus on how mechanisms like shadowbanning, algorithmic throttling, and the intentional hiding of external links are used not just for profit, but to manage and steer the collective narrative by making alternative information increasingly difficult to find or share.
