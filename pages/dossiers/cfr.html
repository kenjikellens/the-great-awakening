Council on Foreign Relations (CFR)

Analyzing the premier US think tank's influence on foreign policy, diplomatic strategy, and high-level cabinet appointments.

Documented Evidence

The Council on Foreign Relations (CFR) is a nonpartisan membership organization, think tank, and publisher. It is widely considered the most influential foreign-policy organization in the United States.

  • Cabinet Representation: Historical analysis of US administrations shows a consistently high percentage of cabinet members and senior advisors who are active CFR members. This spans both Democratic and Republican administrations.
  • Policy Formulation: Through its "David Rockefeller Studies Program" and various task forces, the CFR generates specific policy recommendations on issues ranging from trade and cybersecurity to national security. Its flagship magazine, Foreign Affairs, is the primary platform for debut policy doctrines (e.g., the 1947 "Containment" policy).
  • Corporate Ties: The CFR includes a Corporate Program with over 100 corporate members (including major banks and tech firms), facilitating direct dialogue between the private sector and the policy-making community.

Narrative Context

Within Great Awakening research, the CFR is analyzed as the "Establishment" or the "Actual State Department." Critics argue that the organization acts as a filtering mechanism for leadership, where individuals must align with the CFR's globalist and interventionist outlook before being considered for high-level government positions.

The concern is that the CFR ensures continuity in US foreign policy regardless of which political party is in power, effectively creating a "Deep State" infrastructure where long-term geopolitical strategies—often aligned with international institutions like the UN or WEF—take precedence over national voter interests.
