HAARP

Investigation into the High-frequency Active Auroral Research Program and atmospheric science.

Overview

The **High-frequency Active Auroral Research Program (HAARP)** is a program used to study the properties and behavior of the ionosphere. It utilizes the Ionospheric Research Instrument (IRI), a high-power radio frequency transmitter facility operating in the high frequency (HF) range. While publicly focusing on communications and surveillance research, it has become a central focus for theories regarding atmospheric manipulation.

Documented Facts

  • Facility: Located near Gakona, Alaska, HAARP consists of 180 antennas spread over 33 acres, used to beam high-frequency waves into the upper atmosphere.
  • Ionospheric Heating: The IRI can temporarily excite a limited area of the ionosphere, allowing scientists to study how it affects radio signal propagation and satellite communication.
  • Transfer of Control: In 2015, the facility was transferred from the US Air Force/Navy to the University of Alaska Fairbanks (UAF), transitioning it to a civilian-led research platform.

Narrative Analysis

Independent research analyzes HAARP as a potential Geophysical Weapon. Based on patents like US4686605 (Bernard Eastlund), researchers explore the capability of high-power radio beams to alter weather patterns, trigger earthquakes through crustal resonance, and even influence human brainwaves via ELF (Extremely Low Frequency) waves generated in the ionosphere.

"The ability to heat the ionosphere is the ability to move the jet stream. Moving the jet stream is the ability to control the moisture of entire continents." - Atmospheric Research Note
