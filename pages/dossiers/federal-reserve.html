Federal Reserve System

The 1913 creation of the US central bank and the engine of debt-based finance.

Overview

The **Federal Reserve System** (often called the Fed) is the central bank of the United States. It was created by the Congress to provide the nation with a safer, more flexible, and more stable monetary and financial system. Despite its governmental appearance, its 12 regional banks are organized as private corporations owned by member commercial banks.

Documented Facts

  • The Jekyll Island Meeting: In 1910, a secret meeting took place at the Jekyll Island Club to draft the legislation that would become the Federal Reserve Act. Attendees included high-level bankers like Paul Warburg and Senator Nelson Aldrich.
  • Fiat Currency: Since 1971 (the closing of the Gold Window by Nixon), the Fed has managed a purely fiat currency system, backed by nothing but the trust and credit of the US government.
  • Monopoly on Issue: The Fed has the exclusive right to issue Federal Reserve Notes, which are then "lent" to the government at interest, creating a cycle of inherent national debt.

Narrative Analysis

Academic and independent researchers frequently analyze the Fed as a debt-engine. Because the currency itself is issued as debt, it is mathematically impossible to pay off the total debt, as the interest required for repayment was never created in the initial money supply.
