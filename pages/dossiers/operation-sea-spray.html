Operation Sea-Spray

A declassified US Navy secret experiment involving the biological spraying of the San Francisco Bay Area to simulate a microbial attack.

Overview

In September 1950, the US Navy conducted a secret experiment called Operation Sea-Spray. For six days, a Navy vessel positioned off the coast of San Francisco used giant hoses to spray a massive cloud of bacteria into the city's famous fog. The goal was to determine how vulnerable a large metropolitan area would be to a biological attack. At the time, the military believed the chosen bacteria, Serratia marcescens and Bacillus globigii, were harmless simulators.

Technological Spread

Based on samples taken from 43 monitoring sites across the city, the military determined that San Francisco had received enough of the bacteria for nearly every one of the city's 800,000 residents to have inhaled at least 5,000 of the particles. The experiment confirmed that a sea-borne biological attack could effectively blanket a major city in toxic or infectious agents within hours.

Health Consequences & Exposure

Shortly after the spraying, 11 residents checked into Stanford University Hospital with rare and serious urinary tract infections. One patient, Edward Nevin, died after the infection spread to his heart. The Nevin family would later sue the US government in 1981, after the project was declassified in 1976. However, the court ruled that the government was immune to the lawsuit because the experiment was a "discretionary function" of the military.

Research Note

Operation Sea-Spray is one of hundreds of documented instances where the US military performed experiments on unwitting domestic populations. We cross-reference this with similar tests in the New York City subway system (1966) and the exposure of populations in St. Louis to zinc cadmium sulfide in the 1950s and 60s.
