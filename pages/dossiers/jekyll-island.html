Jekyll Island History: Fed Meeting ↗

Executive Summary

In November 1910, a secret gathering of the world's most powerful bankers and political figures took place at the Jekyll Island Club off the coast of Georgia. The meeting was so secretive that attendees traveled in a private railroad car under assumed names and referred to each other only by first names to avoid detection by the press.

The purpose of this clandestine summit was to draft a plan for a national central bank in the United States. The result was the Aldrich Plan, which provided the structural foundation for the Federal Reserve Act of 1913. This event is considered by researchers to be the pivotal moment where the US monetary system was transitioned into a private-public hybrid model, ensuring a debt-based currency system.

The Conspiracy of Secrecy

The meeting was organized by Senator Nelson Aldrich, chairman of the National Monetary Commission. Aldrich was closely aligned with the Rockefeller and Morgan banking interests. To maintain absolute secrecy, the group utilized a private hunting club on Jekyll Island, which at the time was owned by a group of millionaires including J.P. Morgan himself.

The "First Name Club"

Frank Vanderlip, president of the National City Bank of New York and an attendee, later admitted in his autobiography: "We were told to leave our last names at home. We were to refer to one another as Nelson, Harry, Frank, and so on... We were as secretive as any group of conspirators."

The reason for such extreme secrecy was that the American public was deeply suspicious of a "Money Trust" or a central bank. If it were known that the country's leading bankers were drafting the banking reform legislation themselves, it would have been politically impossible to pass.

The Aldrich Plan & The Fed

The primary architect of the technical plan was Paul Warburg, a partner at Kuhn, Loeb & Co. and a representative of the Rothschild banking interests in Europe. Warburg understood the European central banking models and sought to implement a similar system in the US, but hidden behind a "decentralized" facade to appease political opposition.

  • Core Proposal: A system of regional banks overseen by a central board, with the power to issue currency backed by commercial paper and gold.
  • Political Camouflage: The plan was initially introduced as the "Aldrich Plan" but was rejected due to its association with Wall Street. After the 1912 election, the plan was slightly modified and renamed the Federal Reserve Act under the Wilson administration.
  • Institutional Outcome: The Federal Reserve System was signed into law on December 23, 1913, while many members of Congress were away for the Christmas holiday.
