Operation Gladio

The declassified history of NATO's secret "stay-behind" armies and the Strategy of Tension.

Overview

Operation Gladio (Latin for "sword") was the codename for a clandestine NATO program that established secret "stay-behind" paramilitary networks across Western Europe during the Cold War. While officially designed to conduct guerrilla warfare in the event of a Soviet invasion, investigations have linked these units to domestic political subversion and terrorist attacks aimed at discrediting left-wing movements.

The Strategy of Tension

Historical research, particularly the 1990 Italian parliamentary inquiry, revealed that Gladio-linked assets were involved in the "Strategy of Tension" (Strategia della tensione). This involved committing or facilitating acts of violence—falsely attributed to political opponents—to steer public opinion toward support for authoritarian stability and increased military presence. Key events include:

  • Peteano Bombing (1972): A neo-fascist attack that revealed the existence of secret arms caches.
  • Bologna Massacre (1980): The deadliest terrorist attack in Italian history, linked to P2 masonic lodge and intelligence assets.

Declassified Status: FACT

Initially dismissed as a conspiracy theory, Operation Gladio was officially confirmed by Italian Prime Minister Giulio Andreotti in October 1990. Subsequent disclosures revealed similar networks existed in France, Belgium, West Germany, Greece, and neutral countries like Switzerland. The European Parliament passed a resolution in 1990 condemning these clandestine military organizations as existing outside of any democratic control.
