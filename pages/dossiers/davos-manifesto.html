Executive Summary

The Davos Manifesto is the foundational ethical and regulatory code of the World Economic Forum (WEF). Originally drafted in 1973 and significantly expanded in 2020, the manifesto establishes "Stakeholder Capitalism" as the mandatory operating model for global corporations within the Fourth Industrial Revolution (4IR). It posits that a company’s purpose is to "engage all its stakeholders in shared and sustained value creation," moving beyond the 20th-century focus on shareholder profit alone.

Research conducted by this project identifies the Davos Manifesto not merely as a corporate mission statement, but as the de facto constitution for a trans-border governance system. By defining "society" and "the environment" as stakeholders to whom management is directly accountable, the manifesto provides the ethical justification for corporations to bypass national legislation in favor of global ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) standards.

Historical Context: The 1973 Code of Ethics

The original 1973 Davos Manifesto was drafted during the third European Management Symposium (the precursor to the WEF). At the time, it was a revolutionary document that introduced the concept that professional management must "harmonize" the interests of four key groups: clients, shareholders, workers, and society.

The 1973 version emphasized the role of the manager as a "trustee" for these groups. It was the first formal rejection of the traditional view that the sole purpose of a business is the maximization of owner wealth. This early draft laid the groundwork for the multi-stakeholder governance model that would become the dominant force in global policy integration in the 21st century.

The 2020 Repercussion: The Universal Purpose of a Company

On the 50th anniversary of the WEF, Klaus Schwab released the Davos Manifesto 2020, subtitled "The Universal Purpose of a Company in the Fourth Industrial Revolution." This update was necessary to align corporate behavior with the goals of the Great Reset and Agenda 2030. The 2020 Manifesto is significantly more prescriptive than its predecessor, calling for:

  • Zero Tolerance for Corruption: Establishing global standardization of ethics that supersede local cultural or legal variations.
  • Fair Taxation: A commitment to global tax frameworks (such as the Global Minimum Tax) to prevent profit-shifting and ensure corporate contribution to global "social" funds.
  • Environmental Stewardship: The mandatory transition to a "circular, shared and regenerative economy."
  • Data Ethics: Defining the "ethical and efficient use of data" as a fundamental corporate responsibility in the age of AI.

The 2020 Manifesto explicitly states that executive remuneration must be linked to these stakeholder objectives, ensuring that the global technocratic elite is incentivized to prioritize global sustainability over local or national economic growth.

Stakeholder Capitalism vs. Shareholder Primacy

To understand the significance of the Davos Manifesto, it must be contrasted with the Friedman Doctrine (Shareholder Primacy), which dominated economic thought from the 1970s until 2019. Milton Friedman argued that the "only social responsibility of business is to increase its profits."

The Davos Manifesto represents the total defeat of the Friedman Doctrine. By redefining the "social responsibility" of the corporation, the WEF has enabled a system where non-elected corporate boards make decisions that have profound societal impacts (such as de-banking "non-compliant" industries or implementing digital surveillance) under the justification of "serving society." Research indicates this shift represents a transfer of sovereign power from democratic legislatures to private-public partnerships coordinated in Davos.

The ESG Integration: From Ethics to Enforcement

The Manifesto’s principles are operationalized through ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) metrics. These metrics provide the data-driven framework through which Stakeholder Capitalism is enforced. Corporations are no longer judged by their balance sheets alone, but by a Global Social Credit Score for business.

Key Enforcement Mechanisms:

  • Institutional Investment: Asset managers like BlackRock and Vanguard (the "Big Three") utilize the Davos Manifesto’s definitions to shift trillions of dollars in capital toward "compliant" companies.
  • Supply Chain Proliferation: Large corporations (the "Masters") require their small and medium-sized suppliers to adhere to the Manifesto’s standards, creating a "trickle-down" technocracy that impacts every level of the global economy.
  • Regulatory Capture: National regulators are increasingly adopting WEF-designed ESG reporting standards into law, effectively codifying the Davos Manifesto as the global standard for business operation.
