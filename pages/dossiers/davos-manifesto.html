The Davos Manifesto

The World Economic Forum's ethical guide for modern corporations.

Overview

The **Davos Manifesto 2020** is a set of ethical principles that guide the World Economic Forum's vision for "Stakeholder Capitalism." It replaces the traditional shareholder-first model with a commitment to all stakeholders: society, employees, customers, and the environment. It serves as the philosophical backbone for the Great Reset.

Documented Facts

  • The 1973 Version: Originally drafted in 1973, the first manifesto established the idea that management is not only responsible to shareholders but also to customers, employees, and society.
  • The 2020 Update: Re-issued in 2020 to align with the Fourth Industrial Revolution and the Sustainable Development Goals. It explicitly states that companies should pay their "fair share of taxes" and have "zero tolerance for corruption."
  • Stakeholder Capitalism: Defined as a model where companies do not optimize short-term profits but instead maximize the medium- and long-term value creation.

Narrative Analysis

Researchers analyze the Davos Manifesto as the Corporate Charter for Global Governance. By redefining "stakeholders" to include global communities (as represented by transnational NGOs and boards), the manifesto effectively encourages companies to prioritize globalist goals over local or national interests.

"The purpose of a company is to engage all its stakeholders in shared and sustained value creation... it is the foundation of a global economic system that works for all." - Davos Manifesto 2020 Summary

Research Note: The End of Private Choice

The shift to stakeholder capitalism is interpreted by critics as a way to standardize "ethical" behavior across all companies, essentially creating a cartel of approved businesses that follow the WEF's social and environmental guidelines, regardless of consumer demand.
