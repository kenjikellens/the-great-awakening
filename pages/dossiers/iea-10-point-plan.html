IEA: 10-Point Plan to Cut Oil Use

Documentation on the emergency measures proposed to reduce energy dependence and manage supply crises through societal behavioral changes.

Emergency Context

In March 2022, following the Russian invasion of Ukraine and the subsequent energy market volatility, the International Energy Agency (IEA) released a report outlining "immediate actions" to reduce global oil consumption. The plan aimed to lower demand by 2.7 million barrels a day within four months.

The 10-Point Plan

The report recommends that governments implement the following measures to forcibly or voluntarily reduce energy use:

  • Lower Speed Limits: Reducing highway speed limits by at least 10 km/h.
  • Remote Work: Working from home up to three days a week where possible.
  • Car-Free Sundays: Implementing car-free Sundays in major cities.
  • Public Transport: Making public transport cheaper and incentivizing micro-mobility.
  • Alternate Car Access: Restricting private car use in large cities to even/odd license plate days.
  • Business Travel: Avoiding business travel when alternative communication is available.

"Demand Restraint" & Obligation

A critical aspect of the IEA framework is the concept of "Demand Restraint." Under the International Energy Program (IEP) Agreement, IEA member countries are legally required to have emergency plans in place to reduce oil demand if there is a major supply disruption. The report explicitly mentions that "many of these measures can be implemented as mandatory regulations" during a state of emergency.

Research Note

While often presented as "recommendations," these measures are part of a pre-existing legal framework for IEA member states. Researchers monitor the overlap between these "emergency oil measures" and long-term "climate goals," noting that the infrastructure for one is increasingly being used to justify the other.
