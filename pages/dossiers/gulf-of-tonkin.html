Gulf of Tonkin Incident

Analyzing the fabricated naval confrontation that served as the primary justification for the escalation of the Vietnam War.

Executive Summary

The Gulf of Tonkin Incident refers to two separate naval confrontations off the coast of North Vietnam involving the **USS Maddox** and the **USS Turner Joy**. The incident served as the primary justification for the 1964 Gulf of Tonkin Resolution, which granted President Lyndon B. Johnson nearly unlimited authority to escalate U.S. military involvement in Vietnam without a formal declaration of war.

Research conducted by the Great Awakening project, supported by the 2005 declassification of National Security Agency (NSA) records, confirms that the second, more critical "attack" on August 4, 1964, never occurred. It was a "phantom battle" manufactured through the deliberate distortion of signals intelligence (SIGINT). This case study provides definitive historical proof of how intelligence agencies can liquidate context and withhold evidence to engineer a Casus Belli (justification for war) for the executive branch.

The 1964 Strategy: OPLAN 34A & DESOTO Patrols

The presence of the USS Maddox in the Gulf of Tonkin was part of a secret operation known as OPLAN 34A. This was a program of covert "hit-and-run" raids against North Vietnamese coastal installations. The Maddox was conducting a DESOTO Patrol—an electronic intelligence-gathering mission designed to intercept North Vietnamese communications and trigger their coastal defense radars.

On August 2, 1964, the Maddox was engaged by three North Vietnamese Navy torpedo boats. A skirmish ensued, resulting in minor damage to the U.S. vessel and the sinking of one Vietnamese boat. While this was a real event, it was the direct result of U.S. provocations within North Vietnamese territorial waters. However, the Johnson administration chose to frame the event as "unprovoked aggression" to set the stage for a legislative blank check.

The "Phantom Battle" of August 4: The Hanyok Study

In 2005, a declassified history by NSA historian Robert J. Hanyok titled "Skunks, Bogies, Silent Hounds, and the Flying Fish" revealed that the reported "second attack" on August 4 was nonexistent. Hanyok’s study concluded that NSA analysts had "bent" the evidence to suit the administration's needs.

Key Findings of the NSA Audit:

  • 90% Withholding: Intelligence officers withheld roughly 90% of the relevant SIGINT from the decision-makers, providing only the fragments that suggested an attack.
  • Temporal Translation: Analysts shifted the timestamps of Vietnamese intercepts to make it appear that they were preparing an attack on August 4, when they were actually discussing the aftermath of the August 2 skirmish.
  • Fabricated Sonar Reports: The "attacks" were actually misinterpreted sonar signals caused by poor weather and the wake of the U.S. ships themselves (the "Flying Fish" effect).

Despite having serious doubts within hours of the report, Secretary of Defense Robert McNamara and President Johnson proceeded to order "retaliatory" airstrikes and pushed the Resolution through Congress.

The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution: Legislative Blank Check

The Gulf of Tonkin Resolution was passed on August 7, 1964, with nearly unanimous support (only two dissenting votes in the Senate). It authorized the President to "take all necessary measures to repel any armed attack against the forces of the United States and to prevent further aggression."
