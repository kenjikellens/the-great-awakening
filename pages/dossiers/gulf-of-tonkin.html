Gulf of Tonkin Incident

The 1964 naval events used to justify the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution and the escalation of the Vietnam War.

Overview

The **Gulf of Tonkin Incident** describes two separate confrontations involving North Vietnamese torpedo boats and US destroyers off the coast of North Vietnam. The events led to the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution, which granted President Lyndon B. Johnson the authority to assist any Southeast Asian country whose government was considered to be jeopardized by "communist aggression."

Documented Facts

  • August 2 Incident: On August 2, 1964, the USS Maddox was engaged by three North Vietnamese Navy torpedo boats. A sea battle ensued, and the US suffered minor damage.
  • August 4 Fabrication: On August 4, a second attack was reported. However, later analysis by the US government and the National Security Agency (NSA) concluded that the second attack **never took place**. It was likely a result of misinterpreted sonar signals and weather conditions.
  • The Resolution: Within days of the second reported "attack," the US Congress passed the Gulf of Tonkin Resolution with nearly unanimous support, authorizing a full-scale military conflict.

Narrative Analysis

The Gulf of Tonkin incident is a landmark case study for **fabricated casus belli**. Researchers analyze how the administration used inconclusive data to create a public perception of unprovoked aggression, allowing for the massive escalation of a war that would last another decade.

"The review of the evidence for the second 'attack' reveals that it was a combination of poorly read radar, nervous sonar operators, and a desire by the administration to have a reason for escalation." - NSA Declassified Review Summary (2005)
