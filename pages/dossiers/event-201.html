Event 201

A high-level pandemic exercise hosted months before the COVID-19 outbreak.

Overview

Event 201 was a 3.5-hour pandemic tabletop exercise that simulated a series of dramatic, scenario-based facilitated discussions regarding response to a hypothetical, but scientifically plausible, pandemic. It aimed to identify areas where public-private partnerships will be necessary during the response to a severe pandemic in order to diminish large-scale economic and societal consequences.

Scenario Details

The exercise simulated an outbreak of a novel zoonotic coronavirus (CAPS) transmitted from bats to pigs to people that eventually becomes efficiently transmissible from person to person, leading to a severe pandemic. The pathogen and the disease it caused were modeled largely on SARS, but it was more transmissible in the community setting by people with mild symptoms.

Key Recommendations

The Event 201 exercise resulted in seven key recommendations for public and private sector leaders:

  • Governments, international organizations, and businesses should plan now for how essential corporate capabilities will be utilized during a large-scale pandemic.
  • Industry, national governments, and international organizations should work together to enhance internationally held stockpiles of medical countermeasures (MCMs).
  • Countries, international organizations, and global transportation companies should work together to maintain travel and commerce during severe pandemics.
  • Governments should provide more resources and support for the development and surge manufacturing of vaccines, therapeutics, and diagnostics.
  • Global business should recognize the economic burden of pandemics and fight for greater preparedness.

Research Note: Narrative Context

Event 201 is frequently cited by independent researchers due to its timing—occurring just months before the official identification of COVID-19—and its focus on "countering misinformation" and the role of social media platforms in controlling the narrative during a health crisis. The simulation included specific discussions on how to manage "fake news" and "disinformation" through algorithmic control and government-media partnerships.
