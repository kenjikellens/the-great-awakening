ESG Scores

Environmental, Social, and Governance metrics and the restructuring of capital.

Overview

Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) scores are a set of standards used by socially conscious investors to screen potential investments. Environmental criteria consider how a company performs as a steward of nature. Social criteria examine how it manages relationships with employees, suppliers, customers, and the communities where it operates. Governance deals with a company’s leadership, executive pay, audits, internal controls, and shareholder rights.

Documented Facts

The term "ESG" was first popularized in a 2004 UN report titled Who Cares Wins, which argued that embedding environmental, social, and governance factors in capital markets makes good business sense.

Non-Standardization: Unlike traditional credit ratings, ESG scores lack a universal framework. Providers like MSCI, Sustainalytics, and S&P use different weightings, leading to "Aggregate Confusion" where the same company can be rated both "AAA" and "CCC" across different agencies.

Narrative Analysis

From a research perspective, ESG scores are analyzed as the infrastructure for a Global Social Credit System for corporations. By tying access to capital to ideological compliance, transnational bodies can enforce social and environmental policies that bypass the democratic legislative process.

"ESG is the tool they are using to force companies to do things that have nothing to do with their business model, but everything to do with a political agenda." - Research Summary

ESG is the operational arm of the WEF's "Stakeholder Capitalism," moving from a model of shareholder accountability to a model where "stakeholders" (often self-appointed transnational groups) determine a company's success based on its alignment with global goals.
