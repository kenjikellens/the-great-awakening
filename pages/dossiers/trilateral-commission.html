Executive Summary

The Trilateral Commission is a non-governmental, policy-oriented discussion group founded in 1973 by David Rockefeller. Its primary purpose is to foster closer cooperation between the "Three Regions"—North America, Europe, and the Asia-Pacific (specifically Japan and now China/India)—on challenges facing the global economic and security order. While marketed as a benign forum for dialogue, research conducted by the Great Awakening project identifies the Commission as the **Institutional Blueprint for Global Technocracy**.

As of 2026, the Trilateral Commission continues to manage the transition of national governance into a more centralized, "interdependent" framework. By bringing together the heads of the world’s most powerful financial institutions (Chase, Goldman Sachs) with former presidents, intelligence directors, and high-ranking military officers, the Commission ensures that the Fourth Industrial Revolution is managed by a transnational elite rather than through the unpredictable processes of democratic debate.

The Technetronic Era: Brzezinski’s Intellectual Framework

The intellectual foundation of the Trilateral Commission is found in the work of its first Director, Zbigniew Brzezinski, specifically his 1970 book "Between Two Ages: America's Role in the Technetronic Era." Brzezinski argued that the international system was moving into an age where computers and telecommunications would allow for a Systemic Micro-Management of the population.

In this "Technetronic Era," traditional democratic participation would be replaced by **Expert-Led Administration**. Brzezinski posited that the complexity of the global economy required a "Global Social Control" mechanism that was beyond the comprehension of the average citizen. The Trilateral Commission was designed to be the vehicle for this transition, aligning the "enlightened" leaders of the three regions to manage the "Global Commons" (resources, energy, and digital data) through a unified technological standard.

The 1973 Foundation: Atlantic-Pacific Consolidation

The Trilateral Commission was formally launched at a meeting in 1973, following a series of private discussions at the 1972 Bilderberg Meeting in Knokke, Belgium. David Rockefeller, then Chairman of Chase Manhattan Bank, became the primary benefactor. The objective was to bring Japan—then a surging economic power—into the Western security and financial fold, creating a "Trilateral" alliance that could manage the post-Gold Standard world.

Critically, the Commission was the first major elite forum to include significant representation from the private banking sector at the highest level of policy formation. Research identifies this as the moment the Public-Private Hybrid model of governance became the global standard. The Commission’s role was to ensure that the "rules-based order" favored the expansion of multinational corporations over the protectionist interests of sovereign nation-states.

Case Study: The Trilateral "Takeover" of 1976

The most historical documented instance of Trilateral influence occurred during the 1976 US Presidential Election. Jimmy Carter, an obscure governor from Georgia, was selected by the Trilateral Commission as a member in 1973. Following his election, Carter appointed over **20 Trilateral members** to his administration, including:

  • Zbigniew Brzezinski: National Security Advisor.
  • Cyrus Vance: Secretary of State.
  • Paul Volcker: Chairman of the Federal Reserve.
  • Walter Mondale: Vice President.

This administration oversaw the significant "deregulation" of the banking sector and the formalization of the Petrodollar system, solidifying the Fed’s global role. For researchers, the 1976 administration serves as the definitively declassified case study for how the Commission "confirms" and "places" political candidates into executive power.

Current Status: 2026 & The Indo-Pacific Pivot

In 2026, the Trilateral Commission has pivoted toward the Indo-Pacific as the primary theater for digital and financial governance. With the inclusion of members from India and China, the Commission is focused on the Interoperability of Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI).
