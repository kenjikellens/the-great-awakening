Bohemian Grove

Analyzing the historical and sociological significance of the private men's club and its annual gathering of the North American power elite.

Documented Evidence

Bohemian Grove is a 2,700-acre campground in Monte Rio, California, belonging to a private San Francisco-based gentlemen's club known as the Bohemian Club. Every July, the club hosts a two-week encampment for some of the most prominent men in the world.

  • Institutional Archives: Detailed records of the club's history, including programs, scripts for their "Cremation of Care" ceremony, and photographs, are held in the Bancroft Library at UC Berkeley and the San Francisco Public Library.
  • Political Attendance: Official records from presidential libraries (including Reagan and Nixon) document invitations and attendance of US Presidents at the Grove. It is historically significant as the site of a 1942 planning meeting for the Manhattan Project.
  • Chatham House Rule: The club operates with extreme confidentiality. Its motto, "Weaving Spiders Come Not Here," suggests that business and political deals should not be conducted, though the high concentration of power makes it a primary subject for sociological study of elite networks.

Narrative Context

Researchers in the Great Awakening community focus on Bohemian Grove as a symbol of "High Technocracy" and "Occult Governance." The theatrical and ritualistic aspects of the encampment, particularly the "Cremation of Care" ceremony performed before a massive stone owl, are analyzed as ritualized bonding exercises for the global ruling class.

The core concern is the lack of transparency: when the leaders of the nuclear, financial, and political sectors meet in total secrecy to participate in mock-rituals and private "Lakeside Talks," it raises fundamental questions about where actual policy is debated and decided—outside the reach of public scrutiny and democratic institutions.
