Club of Rome

Analyzing the influential global think tank that pioneered the "Malthusian" approach to global resource management and environmental limits.

Documented Evidence

The Club of Rome is a non-profit, informal organization of intellectuals and business leaders whose objective is to "act as a global catalyst for change through identification and analysis of the crucial problems facing humanity."

  • The Limits to Growth (1972): This report utilized computer modeling to predict the collapse of civilization by the 21st century due to exponential economic and population growth against finite resource limits. It laid the intellectual foundation for the modern degrowth and environmental movements.
  • The First Global Revolution (1991): In this follow-up report, the Council of the Club of Rome discussed the need for a common enemy to unite humanity. The document famously states: "In searching for a new enemy to unite us, we came up with the idea that pollution, the threat of global warming, water shortages, famine and the like would fit the bill... All these dangers are caused by human intervention... The real enemy then, is humanity itself."
  • Global Governance: The Club of Rome has consistently advocated for a "world government" or "new global order" to manage the systemic crises identified in their computer models.

Narrative Context

In Great Awakening research, the Club of Rome is identified as the ideological laboratory for the "Great Reset." Critics argue that by framing humanity and human development as a "cancer" or the "enemy," the organization provides the moral and scientific justification for radical population reduction and the implementation of a technocratic planned economy.

The 1991 admission regarding the "idea" of using global warming as a unifying enemy is frequently cited as evidence that modern climate policy is a manufactured crisis designed to achieve political unification and centralized control over all human activity.
