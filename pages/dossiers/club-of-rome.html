Club of Rome

The think tank that pioneered the global ecological limits narrative.

Overview

The **Club of Rome** is an international think tank composed of current and former heads of state, UN bureaucrats, high-level politicians, government officials, diplomats, scientists, economists, and business leaders from around the globe. It was founded in 1968 at the Accademia dei Lincei in Rome and gained world fame with its first report, The Limits to Growth (1972).

Critical Publications

The Limits to Growth (1972): This report used computer modeling to predict that economic growth could not continue indefinitely because of the limited availability of natural resources. It introduced the concept of "overshoot" and argued for a state of global equilibrium.

The First Global Revolution (1991): This report analyzed the state of the world after the Cold War and argued that humanity is in the midst of a global revolution as it transitions to a new era.

The "Common Enemy" Quote

One of the most significant and frequently cited passages from The First Global Revolution (page 75) discusses the need for a unifying threat to achieve global cooperation:

"In searching for a common enemy against whom we can unite, we came up with the idea that pollution, the threat of global warming, water shortages, famine and the like, would fit the bill... in designating these dangers as the enemy, we fall into the trap... namely mistaking symptoms for causes. All these dangers are caused by human intervention... the real enemy then is humanity itself."

Research Note: Malthusian Influence

The Club of Rome is often viewed by independent researchers as the intellectual foundation for modern neo-Malthusianism. Their emphasis on resource scarcity and the "common threat" of environmental decay is analyzed as a strategic narrative designed to justify centralized global management of resources and population control measures.
