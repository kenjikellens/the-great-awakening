IMF & World Bank

The Bretton Woods institutions and the coordination of the global debt-order.

Overview

The International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank were created at the 1944 Bretton Woods Conference. The IMF focuses on international monetary cooperation and financial stability, while the World Bank focuses on long-term economic development and poverty reduction. Together, they form the cornerstone of the post-WWII international financial architecture.

Documented Facts

  • Standardizing the Dollar: The Bretton Woods system originally pegged the US Dollar to Gold and other currencies to the Dollar, establishing the US as the world’s reserve currency issuer.
  • Structural Adjustment: To receive loans, countries must often implement "Structural Adjustment Programs" (SAPs), which mandate austerity measures, privatization of state industries, and market liberalization.
  • Conditionality: Loans are not grants; they are contingent on the recipient nation adhering to the "Washington Consensus" policies favored by the institutions.

Narrative Analysis

Research within the archive analyzes the IMF and World Bank as tools for Debt-Trap Diplomacy on a global scale. By providing loans that are often impossible to repay, these institutions gain the power to dictate the internal economic policies and resource management of sovereign nations.

"Structural adjustment is the modern method of colonization without a single soldier on the ground." - Economic Critique Summary

Research Note: The "Rules-Based" Order

The IMF and World Bank are essential for maintaining the "internationally agreed rules-based order." Independent analysis focuses on how "the rules" typically favor the needs of transnational capital and the "Big Three" investment firms over local and national economic independence.
