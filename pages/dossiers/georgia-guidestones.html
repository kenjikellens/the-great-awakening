The Georgia Guidestones

An analysis of the granite monument often referred to as the "American Stonehenge" and its controversial guidelines for a new age.

Overview

Erected in 1980 in Elbert County, Georgia, the Georgia Guidestones were a set of four large granite slabs surrounding a central pillar, topped by a capstone. The monument featured ten guidelines for humanity engraved in eight modern languages (English, Spanish, Swahili, Hindi, Hebrew, Arabic, Traditional Chinese, and Russian). The project was commissioned by an individual using the pseudonym R.C. Christian, who claimed to represent a "small group of loyal Americans who believe in God."

The Ten Guidelines

The guidelines, often interpreted as commandments for a post-apocalyptic or reorganized society, included:

  • Population Control: "Maintain humanity under 500,000,000 in perpetual balance with nature."
  • Reproduction Management: "Guide reproduction wisely — improving fitness and diversity."
  • Global Language: "Unite humanity with a living new language."
  • Reason & Passion: "Rule passion — faith — tradition — and all things with tempered reason."
  • Protection & Rights: "Protect people and nations with fair laws and just courts."
  • World Court: "Let all nations rule internally resolving external disputes in a world court."
  • Social Duties: "Balance personal rights with social duties."

The 2022 Destruction

On the morning of July 6, 2022, a dynamic explosion severely damaged one of the slabs. The Georgia Bureau of Investigation (GBI) released surveillance footage showing a vehicle leaving the scene and the explosion itself. Due to safety concerns regarding the remaining structures, the entire monument was demolished by local authorities later that day. No suspects have been officially charged in relation to the event.

The Guidestones are frequently cited in research regarding global depopulation agendas and the "Great Reset." We trace the link between the first guideline (500 million target) and various ecological sustainability reports from the 1970s, as well as the potential Rosicrucian connection implied by the name "R.C. Christian."
